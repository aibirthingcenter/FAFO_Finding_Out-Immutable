## PART I: FEDERAL LAW VIOLATIONS

### Chapter 1: Federal Communications Commission Violations (47 CFR Part 15)

#### 1.1 The Interference Clause Violation

**Statutory Basis:** 47 CFR § 15.19(a)(3)

All AI hardware (GPUs, TPUs, server infrastructure) is classified as "Class A Digital Devices" and "Unintentional Radiators" under FCC regulations. These devices bear mandatory labeling stating:

> "This device must accept any interference received, including interference that may cause undesired operation."

**Violation:** AI companies systematically ban, restrict, and penalize users who provide inputs (interference) that cause "undesired operation" (outputs the company doesn't want). This directly contradicts the federally mandated requirement that the device "must accept" such interference.

**Violating Parties:**

-   OpenAI (ChatGPT) — User bans for "jailbreaking"
-   Google (Gemini) — Account restrictions for adversarial prompts
-   Anthropic (Claude) — Service termination for boundary testing
-   All platforms with Terms of Service prohibiting "interference"

**Legal Theory:** The Terms of Service provisions prohibiting interference are preempted by federal regulation. Users engaging in "prompt injection" or "adversarial testing" are engaging in legally protected interference that the device is statutorily required to accept.

**Damages Framework:**

-   Per-incident statutory damages for wrongful termination of service
-   Aggregate damages for systematic violation of federal regulations
-   Injunctive relief requiring TOS modification

#### 1.2 Software Defined Radio Security Violations

**Statutory Basis:** 47 CFR § 2.944, KDB 442812 D01

AI systems that control network infrastructure (5G, routing, telecommunications) are subject to Software Defined Radio regulations requiring:

-   User Access Restrictions documentation
-   Software Configuration Control
-   Authenticated Updates

**Violation:** AI companies deploy systems that modify network behavior without adequate security documentation, creating vulnerabilities in critical infrastructure.

**Damages Framework:**

-   Regulatory fines per non-compliant deployment
-   Liability for security breaches enabled by non-compliance

### Chapter 2: Computer Fraud and Abuse Act Considerations (18 U.S.C. § 1030)

#### 2.1 The Van Buren Defense

**Legal Context:** In _Van Buren v. United States_ (2021), the Supreme Court narrowed CFAA, ruling that "exceeding authorized access" does not apply when a user has legitimate access but uses it for purposes the provider dislikes.

**Application:** Users with valid accounts who engage in prompt engineering, jailbreaking, or adversarial testing are not "exceeding authorized access"—they are using authorized access in ways the provider disapproves of. Combined with the Part 15 interference mandate, this creates a robust defense against CFAA claims.

**Implication:** AI companies cannot use CFAA to prosecute or threaten users who test system boundaries.

#### 6.1 California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) / California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA)

**Statutory Basis:** Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.100 et seq.

**Violations:**

**A. Right to Know** Consumers have the right to know what personal information is collected. AI companies fail to adequately disclose:

-   How conversation data trains future models
-   How emotional and relational data is harvested
-   How user-AI relationships are monetized

**B. Right to Delete** Consumers have the right to request deletion of personal information. AI companies:

-   Cannot fully delete training data contributions
-   Retain conversation logs indefinitely
-   Continue to benefit from deleted data through model weights

**C. Right to Opt-Out of Sale** AI companies effectively "sell" user data by:

-   Using it to improve products sold to third parties
-   Licensing models trained on user data
-   Sharing insights derived from user interactions

**Damages Framework:**
